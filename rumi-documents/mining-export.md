# 🌍 Why Rumi Exists

Mining in South America has always been a source of immense wealth — but also of exploitation, undocumented extraction, and ecological damage.  
In Peru, countless stones and minerals have been traded informally, bypassing provenance checks and export laws. This creates risks of:

- **Loss of cultural heritage** through undocumented removal of artisanal or sacred stones.  
- **Economic leakage** when minerals are sold without tax registration or concession oversight.  
- **Environmental harm** from informal mining operations outside compliance frameworks.  
- **Provenance gaps** where specimens circulate without locality labels or documentation, leaving museums and collectors with unlabeled stones.  

Rumi exists to **anchor provenance and compliance** on the blockchain, ensuring stones and minerals are legally sourced, properly documented, and transparently traded.

# ⚖️ Complexity of Export Laws in Peru

Peru’s mining framework is governed by the **Ley General de Minería (Supreme Decree Nº 014-92-EM, 1992)**.  
Exporting stones and minerals requires navigating multiple layers of compliance:

- **Concession ID (INGEMMET)** — proves the stone was extracted from a legal concession.  
- **REINFO registration** — confirms artisanal miners are formalized under national law.  
- **RUC number (SUNAT tax ID)** — ensures sellers are registered with Peru’s tax authority.  
- **HS Code (Harmonized System Code)** — The international classification number assigned to each type of stone or mineral. It ensures customs authorities know exactly what category the product belongs to, aligning Peru’s exports with global trade standards and preventing misclassification.
- **VUCE COD (Código de Operación de Documento)** — The unique identifier generated by Peru’s VUCE system when an export declaration is filed. It links the shipment to its HS Code, RUC, and supporting documents, making the export traceable, legally valid, and required for customs clearance.
- **Environmental & labor compliance** — mandatory for all exporters.  
- **Cultural heritage restrictions** — certain stones/artifacts cannot be exported at all.  

This provenance verification is essential. Without it, stones risk being rejected at customs or flagged as illegal exports.  
Rumi integrates these requirements by linking stones directly to **concession records, REINFO status, and export codes**, automating compliance and making provenance immutable.

# ⛏️ Mining in South America: Historical Overview

## Colonial Era (1500s–1800s)
- **[Potosí silver mines](ca://s?q=Potosi_silver_mines_history)** in Bolivia supplied Spain with immense wealth.  
- **[Huancavelica mercury mines](ca://s?q=Huancavelica_mercury_mines_history)** in Peru enabled silver refining.  
- Indigenous and African laborers endured forced labor under the **mita system**, with severe health impacts.

## 19th Century

### Precious Stones & Collectibles
- **Brazilian gemstones** (amethyst, topaz, tourmaline) became prized in European collections.  
- **Peruvian minerals** (chrysocolla, pyrite, silver ores) were exported as both industrial inputs and collectible specimens.  
- **Chilean lapis lazuli** gained recognition as a rare decorative stone outside Afghanistan.  

### Scientific & Cultural Impact
- Growth of **mineral collecting** among scientists and aristocrats fueled demand for South American specimens.  
- Museums and universities catalogued stones with emphasis on **provenance and locality labels**.  
- Mineralogical societies created exchange networks, linking South America to global science.  
- Many specimens circulated without documentation, reinforcing the need for modern provenance systems like Rumi.

## 20th Century
- **[Copper mining](ca://s?q=Chile_copper_mining_history)** in Chile became dominant, later nationalized.  
- Expansion of **gold and silver mining** in Peru, Brazil, and Colombia.  
- Mining linked to labor movements, strikes, and environmental protests.

## 21st Century
- South America leads in **[lithium triangle](ca://s?q=Lithium_triangle_South_America)** (Argentina, Bolivia, Chile).  
- **Peru and Chile** are top copper producers; **Brazil** dominates iron ore.  
- Mining is central to the global energy transition but raises ecological and social conflicts.
